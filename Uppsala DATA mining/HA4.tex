\subparagraph{c)}
The $p_{value}$ of the Shapiro test is uniformly distributed, as the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test suggests.
\begin{verbatim}
P=c(NA,3000)
for (i in seq(1:3000)){
x=rnorm(40,0,1)
P[i]<-shapiro.test(x)$p}
ks.test(P, "punif")
        One-sample Kolmogorov-Smirnov test

data:  P 
D = 0.0241, p-value = 0.6081
alternative hypothesis: two-sided 
\end{verbatim}
Since the null hypothesis is true ($p_{value}$ of the Shapiro test is truly uniformly distributed), it is also true that the $p_{value}$ of the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test is uniformly distributed.

\subparagraph{c)}
We have strong argument against the null hypothesis that the $p_{value}$ of the Shapiro test is uniformly distributed, as the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test suggests.
\begin{verbatim}
P=c(NA,3000)
for (i in seq(1:3000)){
x=rlaplace(40,0,1/sqrt(2))
P[i]<-shapiro.test(x)$p}
        One-sample Kolmogorov-Smirnov test

data:  P 
D = 0.4563, p-value < 2.2e-16
alternative hypothesis: two-sided 
\end{verbatim}
\subparagraph{c)}
For the laplace distribution and sample of size 40, the normality distribution is very likely to be rejected. We also assessed that the $p_{value}$ is uniformly random if the sample is drawn from a normal distribution.
